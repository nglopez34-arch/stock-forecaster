## 1. Understanding Your Target (and Why It’s Good)

You defined:

[
y_t = \log\left(\sum_{i=1}^{H} r_{t+i}^2 + \epsilon\right)
]

This is essentially **log realized variance** over the next (H) minutes.

Why this is excellent:

* (\sum r^2) ≈ **realized variance**
* The log stabilizes scale and makes the distribution closer to Gaussian
* Volatility is **persistent** (high autocorrelation), which ML loves

For “next ~8 hours”:

* If minute data → (H \approx 480)
* Your input window is 6 hours → ~360 minutes

This setup is very standard in academic volatility forecasting.

## 2. What Actually Predicts Volatility?

Before ML, it’s crucial to understand the **data-generating process**.

Volatility exhibits:

* **Clustering**: high vol follows high vol
* **Long memory**: effects persist across hours/days
* **Asymmetry**: negative returns increase future volatility more than positive ones
* **Microstructure effects**: volume, trade count, spread proxies matter at high frequency

Your NN must learn:

1. **Recent realized volatility**
2. **Intraday seasonality**
3. **Price shocks**
4. **Liquidity & activity levels**

Raw OHLCV alone is *not* enough—you need **derived features**.

---

## 3. Feature Engineering (This Matters More Than Architecture)

### A. Returns-Based Features (Most Important)

Compute **log returns** at multiple aggregation levels:

**Minute returns**
[
r_t = \log(P_t) - \log(P_{t-1})
]

Then derive:

* ( |r_t| ) (absolute return)
* ( r_t^2 ) (instantaneous variance proxy)

**Rolling realized volatility**
Over multiple horizons:

* 5 min
* 15 min
* 60 min
* 180 min
* 360 min

These give the model **multi-scale memory**.

> Why? Volatility is *scale-invariant*. Patterns at 5 minutes and 3 hours both matter.

---

### B. Range-Based Volatility (Very Powerful)

From OHLC:

* **Parkinson volatility** (uses high–low)
* **Garman–Klass** (uses open, high, low, close)

These capture **intrabar information** that close-to-close returns miss.

Neural nets love these because they’re *less noisy* than raw returns.

---

### C. Volume & Trade Activity (Liquidity Proxies)

From your data:

* Volume
* Trade count
* VWAP

Derive:

* Volume returns: ( \Delta \log(\text{volume}) )
* Trades per minute
* Volume imbalance (if available)
* Rolling averages & z-scores

Why?

> Volatility and liquidity are tightly coupled.
> High activity often precedes volatility spikes.

---

### D. Price-Level & Trend Context

Volatility depends on *where you are in price space*.

Useful features:

* Distance from VWAP
* Rolling z-score of price
* Trend strength (e.g., rolling return / rolling volatility)

This helps the model distinguish:

* **Breakouts** vs **mean-reversion noise**

---

### E. Intraday Seasonality (Critical)

Volatility has a **strong time-of-day pattern**.

Encode:

* Minute-of-day (as sine/cosine)
* Hour-of-day (sin/cos)

This avoids giving the NN a false “calendar clock”.

> Without this, the model will misinterpret systematic volatility patterns as randomness.

---

### F. Feature Summary (Concrete List)

For each minute (t), your input tensor might include:

**Core**

* Returns, abs returns, squared returns
* Rolling RV (5, 15, 60, 180, 360)

**OHLC-derived**

* High–low range
* Garman–Klass volatility

**Liquidity**

* Volume
* Trade count
* VWAP distance
* Rolling z-scores

**Context**

* Price z-score
* Trend strength

**Time**

* sin/cos(minute-of-day)

Expect **30–80 features per timestep**.

## 4. Model Architecture: What Actually Works

### Baseline (Do This First)

**Linear HAR model**

HAR = Heterogeneous Autoregressive:
[
RV_{t+1} = \beta_1 RV_{t}^{(5)} + \beta_2 RV_{t}^{(60)} + \beta_3 RV_{t}^{(360)}
]

Why?

* Sets a *hard-to-beat* baseline
* Helps you understand signal vs noise

If your NN can’t beat HAR → something’s wrong.

---

### Recommended NN Architecture (Best Tradeoff)

#### **Temporal CNN (TCN)** or **1D CNN**

This is what I’d recommend for you.

**Why CNNs over LSTMs?**

* Faster
* More stable
* Better at local patterns
* Easier to train
* Less overfitting

Structure:

```
Input: (360 timesteps × F features)

→ 1D Conv (kernel=3–7, causal)
→ ReLU
→ LayerNorm
→ Residual blocks (3–6)
→ Global average pooling
→ Dense
→ Output y_t
```

This lets the network:

* Learn volatility bursts
* Capture multi-scale structure
* Respect time ordering

---

### When to Use LSTM / Transformer

**LSTM**:

* OK, but often overkill
* Harder to train
* Sensitive to hyperparameters

**Transformer**:

* Only worth it if:

  * You have **millions** of samples
  * You know how to regularize well

For now: **TCN/CNN > LSTM > Transformer**

---

## 5. Training Setup (Very Important)

### Loss Function

Use **MSE** on your log-variance target.

Optionally:

* Huber loss (robust to spikes)

---

### Normalization

* Normalize **features only**
* Never normalize targets using future info
* Use rolling or training-only statistics

---

### Train/Test Split

**Strictly chronological**:

* No shuffling
* No leakage
* Walk-forward validation

---

### Evaluation Metrics

* MSE / MAE on log variance
* Correlation with realized volatility
* Compare vs:

  * HAR
  * EWMA
  * GARCH (optional)

If your NN beats HAR by **5–10%**, that’s already excellent.

---

## 6. Common Beginner Mistakes (Avoid These)

❌ Predicting raw volatility instead of log-vol
❌ No intraday seasonality features
❌ Too many layers for too little data
❌ Not using a baseline model
❌ Leakage via normalization or rolling windows
❌ Overfitting to price direction (volatility ≠ returns)
